Loss Leaders

Do you ever do something even though you know it's bad for you? What's your vice— Chocolate? Netflix?

Well, I have a worse habit— using products that are sold at a loss to me.

It starts off so innocently. You and your friend are waiting for the bus one day, and it gets delayed. He glances down at his phone for a second, then turns to you and says:

"Why not take an Uber? This bus costs 8$ for each of us. Look, with my promo, a ride will only be 15$. If we split, it'll actually be cheaper."

What your friend is saying makes sense. Who knows how long the bus will take? And it's literally cheaper. You'd be an idiot not to call this Uber.

So you ride in comfort to your destination, ignoring the voice in the back of your head. Because deep down, there's a part of you that's deeply confused about this situation. It can sense that there's something deeply wrong about this situation.

The source of its confusion is a simple question:

How can someone picking you up in their own car and driving you to your destination be cheaper than a bus, when the bus itself operates at a loss? How is Uber making any money off of this?

The answer: They aren't. Uber has raised a lot of money from VCs, and they are practically paying you to take this ride. Their hope that you'll eventually build the habit of taking Ubers and a few dollars increase in price won't dissuade you from taking them in the future.

They've punked you, like they've punked me, into building a habit.

Now, when prices rise, you have two choices:

  1. Pay a premium to keep the inflated lifestyle you've been experiencing.
  1. Have the comfort that you've come to expect ripped out of your hands and get used to taking the bus again.

Now, you're a pretty savvy person, and you pride yourself with being at least a bit frugal. So you cut back on your Ubers, and take the bus again. It's sucks, but life goes on.

Now, the problem with me is that I never learn my lesson.

  • It happened to me with Uber. (Rides cheaper than public transit? Hell yes!)
  • It happened to me with DoorDash. (Delivery for 1$? Hell yes!)
  • And just recently, it happened to me with Bay Wheels (limitless e-Bikes 15$ a month? Hell yes!)

And this Bay Wheels one hurts really bad, guys.

When Bay Wheels brought back e-Bikes, I knew that the 15$ a month pricing structure would not last. I expected the monthly price to at least double, maybe triple. I knew that they were selling me these rides at a loss, but I was prepared to pay more when the honeymoon ended.

It was way worse that I thought, though. Last month, I took 50 dockless rides. Under the new pricing scheme, I'd have spent $250 on these rides¹ (~1600% increase.)

Looks like I'm getting used to pedaling again, and maybe I'll finally learn my lesson this time.

If a product is severely underpriced, it's a trap. Maybe you are the product. Or, maybe it's a loss leader. But you're paying one way or another, eventually.
